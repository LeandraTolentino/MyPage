Mathematics: Concrete or Abstract?

Mathematics is often seen as an immense abstraction by its detractors - and some zealots. However, Mathematical abstractions exist only because of concrete ideas, and often even physical matter. Without the necessity for humans to travel we would never have come up with the idea of distance, and the triangle inequality Wikipedia Link that naturally follows from this notion. Without computers the Mandelbrot set Wikipedia Link would never have been discovered. Without advances in computing hardware and algorithmic techniques, Adrien Douady, and John H. Hubbard might have never shown contrary to Mandelbrot's belief that the Mandelbrot set was connected Wikipedia Link for Connected
Douady and Hubbard's notes on Dynamics including a proof that the Mandelbrot set is connected. .

The late and great Vladimir ArnoldOne of the greatest Mathematicians of the 20th century, he wrote a controversial polemic that denounced the French school of Mathematics centred around the Bourbaki group.
While the source of his concerns is understandable, it comes off as somewhat biased.
Arnold's polemic had some strong ideas about the zealots of abstraction - he paints an image of French students who can quickly churn out lemmas in algebraic geometry yet are not even the faintest familiar with the elliptic curve Wikipedia Link , or the topological classification of surfaces Wikipedia Link for Riemann surfaces
. I believe as well that the damage that the Bourbaki group has done to the study of Mathematics is immense. What is not at issue however is the separation of Mathematics from physics as Arnold suggests. Mathematics is fun precisely because it is an intellectual pursuit that does not always have to be tied to concrete purposes or exact fields. Abstraction allows people to quickly handle problems in every possible domain, concrete or abstract. A mathematician can do computer science, can help figure out the optimal way to plan a city in a certain space, can do physics, a mathematician can do anything they want!

The history and movement of scientific thought can be summarised succinctly as “From the concrete to the abstract, and from the abstract to the concrete.” What Mathematics education and thinking so deeply needs is this movement.
